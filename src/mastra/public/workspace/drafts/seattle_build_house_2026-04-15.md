# Building a House in Seattle, WA — Process, Permits, Timeline, Costs, and Checklist

Prepared: 2026-04-15

This document summarizes the typical process for building a single-family house in the City of Seattle (high-level steps, required permits and approvals, expected timelines, cost guidance, inspections, key agencies, common pitfalls, and next steps).

Quick summary

Building a new single-family house in Seattle requires early research (zoning and site constraints), a pre-application/site visit with SDCI, preparing construction drawings and reports (architect, structural, geotechnical, drainage/stormwater if required), formal intake and plan review with the Seattle Department of Construction & Inspections (SDCI) and partner agencies, paying permit fees, and then managing inspections during construction. Typical permit-review time for a "simple/medium" new home review target is 2–4 weeks for initial review, and complex projects target 8–12 weeks; overall pre-permit timeline commonly runs 3–6 months (often longer with corrections or special reviews). Construction typically takes 9–12 months (varies widely). Expect permit fees (and hourly review fees) plus separate trade permits and utility connection charges; construction costs per square foot vary by market but historic Seattle analyses used ~$125/sf (2018 baseline) — for current bids get local contractor quotes.

1) Key agencies and systems

- Seattle Department of Construction & Inspections (SDCI): primary permitting, plan review, inspections, code interpretation. Main pages: "How do you get a permit?", construction permit pages, and construction inspections.
- Seattle Services Portal: online intake and application/inspection scheduling system.
- Seattle Public Utilities (SPU): sewer, drainage, water connection issues and sometimes drainage/stormwater reviews.
- Seattle City Light: electric service coordination and meter locations.
- Seattle Department of Transportation (SDOT): street/curb cuts, right-of-way, sidewalk/driveway permits and street improvements.
- Other stakeholders: King County (recording), homeowners association/neighbors (notice), private utilities.

2) High-level step-by-step process

1. Research & feasibility (1–4 weeks)
   - Check zoning, allowed uses, setbacks, lot coverage, height limits, and recent permits/violations via SDCI property records and Seattle Services Portal.
   - Identify if the lot is in an Environmental Critical Area (ECA), FEMA floodplain, or other special overlay (this triggers additional reports and PASV).
   - Basic site survey and property corners (hire a surveyor).

2. Pre-application / Pre-Submittal (PASV/PAR and optional Pre-Submittal Conference) (2–8 weeks)
   - Submit Building & Land Use Pre-Application with a site plan and legal description through the Seattle Services Portal. New single-family construction typically requires a Pre-Application Site Visit (PASV) and a Preliminary Assessment Report (PAR).
   - Optional pre-submittal conferences are recommended for complicated sites (ECA, steep slopes, retaining walls, unusual utilities).

3. Design & prepare permit drawings and technical reports (4–16+ weeks)
   - Engage architect and structural engineer to produce plans (floor plans, elevations, structural calcs).
   - Geotechnical report if required (slopes, ECA, poor soils).
   - Stormwater/drainage plan or checklist if the project disturbs ground or triggers drainage review (SPU/SDCI coordinate).
   - Energy code worksheet/Seattle energy requirements, structural calculations, and civil/site work drawings (if grading, retaining walls, or utility work required).

4. Submit application for intake (intake appointment) (2–3 weeks to intake after submission)
   - Schedule electronic intake through the Seattle Services Portal. SDCI screens submittals; intake typically happens within ~2–3 weeks and you pay ~75% of estimated fees at intake.

5. Plan review by SDCI & partner departments (initial review targets)
   - SDCI plan review types and typical target times:
     - Simple/medium projects: target initial review 2–4 weeks (95% complete within 4 weeks recently)
     - Complex (Full Complex / Full +): target initial review 8–12 weeks (95% complete within ~10–11 weeks recently)
   - Reviews include zoning, structural, drainage, electrical (City Light), SPU, SDOT if needed.
   - Expect one or more correction cycles — each cycle adds time (typically 1–4 weeks each depending on response quality and review backlog).

6. Pay final fees and permit issuance
   - After all corrections are cleared and remaining fees are paid, SDCI issues the permit. Printing approved plans and posting the permit onsite is required before inspections begin.
   - Permits are generally valid 18 months; one-time extension up to 12 months is available if needed.

7. Construction and inspections
   - Construction starts after permit issuance. Schedule inspections through the Seattle Services Portal or by phone (automated inspection line: 206-684-8900). Key inspections often include:
     - Site/erosion control (before ground disturbance)
     - First ground / preconstruction meeting (if required)
     - Foundation (prior to placing concrete)
     - Subfloor (prior to covering floor framing)
     - Framing (prior to insulation/covering)
     - Insulation
     - Mechanical/plumbing/electrical/trade inspections (separate trade permits)
     - Final inspection (approval to occupy / Certificate of Occupancy for non-SF projects)

8. Final approvals, utility hookups, certificates
   - After final inspections and approvals from trades and utilities, you receive final sign-off and can occupy the home.

3) Typical timelines (summary)

- Research & feasibility: 1–4 weeks
- Pre-application & PASV/PAR: 2–8 weeks
- Design and technical reports: 4–16+ weeks
- Intake: 2–3 weeks
- Initial plan review: 2–12 weeks (2–4 weeks typical for simple projects; 8–12 for complex)
- Correction cycles (variable): 2–12+ weeks depending on number and complexity of corrections
- Permit issuance: 1–3 weeks after final payment
- Construction: 6–18 months (typical single-family new build often 9–12 months)

Overall: Expect 3–12+ months before breaking ground (often ~3–6 months for straightforward sites) and 9–18 months from permit to final for most new single-family houses.

4) Costs & fees (what to budget)

- Permit fees: Calculated from project valuation using SDCI fee tables and the fee estimator (you pay ~75% at intake and the rest at issuance). A 5% technology fee is added to charges.
- Plan review & hourly fees: Certain specialized reviews are charged hourly (drainage, geotechnical, some third party reviews). SDCI publishes hourly rates and fee lists.
- Trade permits: Electrical, plumbing, mechanical may be separate permits and fees (often applied for by subcontractors).
- Utility connection and impact fees: SPU and City Light charges for new services or capacity fees may apply.
- Construction hard costs: Historic Seattle FEIS (2018) used ~$125 per square foot for single-family new construction as an indicative baseline; actual 2026 costs may be substantially higher — get current contractor estimates. ADUs/DADUs often have higher $/sf because fixed costs concentrated in small footprints.
- Soft costs: Architecture & engineering (commonly 5–12% of construction cost depending on scope), survey, geotech, permitting, landscaping, contingency (~10–20% recommended), and financing carry costs.
- Example (very rough) estimates:
   - Small remodel/addition permit fees: ~$5,000 (varies)
   - New single-family permit fees: ~$10,000+ (varies by valuation and review time)
   - Construction: highly variable; use local bids. (Seattle FEIS 2018 baseline $125/sf — update with contractor bids)

Note: always use SDCI fee estimator and consult your architect/GC for a precise fee and cost estimate for your project.

5) Inspections (common list)

- Site/erosion control (before disturbance)
- First ground / preconstruction meeting (if required)
- Footings / foundation (before concrete pour)
- Foundation waterproofing / damp proofing (if applicable)
- Subfloor / shear nailing inspection
- Framing inspection
- Rough-in inspections for plumbing, mechanical, electrical (separate trade permits)
- Insulation inspection (energy code compliance)
- Final trade inspections
- Final building inspection and approval

How to schedule: via Seattle Services Portal or automated inspection line (206-684-8900). Inspectors are reachable for questions between 7:30–8:30 a.m. (contact per SDCI guidance).

6) Common pitfalls & risk points

- Environmental Critical Areas (ECA) and steep-slope sites: trigger additional studies, PASV, grading/permitting and can add months and cost.
- Stormwater/drainage requirements: Seattle tightly regulates runoff; small projects can be surprised by drainage requirements or retention solutions.
- Zoning/Setback/Height/ Lot coverage mistakes: early code research pays off; setbacks or lot coverage violations may force redesign.
- Tree protection/permit & removal fees: tree regulations can affect site layout and add requirements.
- Utility coordination and street/sidewalk improvements: SDOT may require frontage improvements or curb/driveway work.
- Incomplete submittals lead to intake delays or multiple correction cycles; using an experienced architect/permit consultant reduces cycles.
- Under-budgeting soft costs and hourly review fees — SDCI charges hourly on some reviews and may bill for additional staff time beyond base fees.

7) Recommended team to hire (minimum for new single-family build)

- Architect (permit drawings, code review, design) — especially useful to minimize corrections.
- Structural engineer (required for structural calculations).
- Civil engineer / drainage designer (if grading, retaining walls, or drainage review required).
- Geotechnical engineer (if ECA, steep slopes, or poor soils).
- Licensed general contractor (GC) or builder, or design-build firm.
- Surveyor (site survey and stakeout).
- Energy consultant or rater (energy code compliance / Seattle specific requirements).
- Landscape architect or arborist (if trees or critical areas involved).
- Permit expeditor (optional) — helpful for complex projects or to manage intake and corrections.
